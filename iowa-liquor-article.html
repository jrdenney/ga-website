  def doEDA(dataframe):
      # Shamelessly yoinked from Ritika Bashker and Chris Messier

      print("Dataframe shape: \n", dataframe.shape) # prints the dataframe size/shape
      print("\nDataframe types: \n", dataframe.dtypes) # prints datatypes in each column
      print("\nDataframe index: \n", dataframe.index) # prints range index
      print("\nDataframe missing values: \n", dataframe.isnull().sum()) # shows missing values per column, if any
      print("\nDataframe header: \n", dataframe.head(10)) # shows first ten rows of dataframe
      print("\nDataframe unique indices: \n") # shows number of unique values in each column

      for ii in dataframe: # prints number of unique entries in each column
          print(ii)
          print(dataframe[ii].nunique())

      for ii in dataframe:
          if dataframe[ii].nunique() == 1:
              # if there's only one unique value, there's no point plotting it
              print("\n{} is homogenous".format(ii))
              continue

          if dataframe[ii].nunique() == dataframe[ii].count():
              # if each item in the column occurs only once, there's also no point plotting it
              print("All items in {} are unique.".format(ii))
              continue

          if dataframe[ii].dtype in [np.int64,np.float64]:
              # if it's a numerical column, plot its distribution
              sns.distplot(dataframe[ii])
          elif dataframe[ii].dtype in [np.object,dt.datetime]:
              # if it's a categorical column, plot its histogram
              sns.countplot(dataframe[ii])

          plt.show()

Record Findings¶

Polk County, home of Des Moines and its suburbs, boasts the largest sales figures in the state - and it isn't even close. Its $7.7 million of sales are more than twice that of the second-top-selling county, Linn County, which is home to Cedar Rapids. The population of the Des Moines metropolitan area is roughly double that of Cedar Rapids, which likely explains the gulf in sales figures.

Quarterly liquor sales hover between $6-7 million during the first and third quarters of 2015 and the first quarter of 2016. Sales experience a nice upward tick in the spring (Q2) and fall (Q4), but it isn't until looking at the monthly data that one sees the reasons why.

Peak purchase months were June, October and December. Since these are not customer purchases, but liquor store purchases, the purpose is to add or renew store stock. June purchases correlate strongly with the upcoming July 4 holiday; October purchases are linked to football season, Halloween, and the upcoming Thanksgiving holiday; December purchases are aimed at Christmas and New Year's Eve celebrations.

What variables are important for total sales projections?¶

Total sales from the year prior are important, but the question that arises is how best to handle them. Obviously the change in quarterly sales from Q1-15 to Q1-16 will pay a role in predicting the sales in quarters 2-4, by comparing the change in Q1 to predict a change in Q2/3/4.

The number of active liquor stores in the state will also have an impact; fewer stores likely means a drop in sales and vice versa. The change in liquor stores from month to respective month per year will impact sales, especially in the fourth quarter.

Volume sold likely confers some impact, but price per volume varies significantly among the various types of liquor.

Study and Methodology¶

The purpose of this study is to utilize 2015 and first quarter (Q1) 2016 Class E liquor sales data to create a predictive model that accurately explains the monthly, quarterly, and yearly sales figures. This model will be used to provide guidance to the Iowa General Assembly as it considers new liquor tax rate legislation.

Additional population data used in this study, including county population by year, was collected from the Iowa State Government (data.iowa.gov). Provided sales data was aggregated at the county level; individual level statistics per county were also calculated (e.g. liquor stores per capita, volume sold per capita). Other data assessed, but not utilized in this model, included bottles per store per month.

A linear regression model was created using first quarter 2015 sales data and trained on the total 2015 sales data per county. It was then tested on first quarter 2016 data to provide a projection of total sales for the 2016 calendar year.

After analyzing the relationship of model variables to the actual 2015 sales figures, it was determined that each variable's correlation with total sales was highly statistically significant and, thus, deemed integral to the model's predictive power.

The results of the 2016 projections show an anticipated yearly revenue of \$339.1 million, an increase of 19% from the \$284.3 million in sales from 2015. This projected increase would provide enough additional state income to potentially table discussions of raising the liquor tax rate by any measure over the next calendar year, a politically advantageous strategy for all parties.

When weighing future considerations for study, it is important to note that store data is currently unreliable, as individual stores open and close for business over the course of a calendar year. This makes predictions at a store level are difficult to make. In addition, using only one year of data creates a dependence and bias towards that particular year, which raises questions of long-term model viability due to a lack of insight into whether a 2015 sales set is indicative of a trend or an aberration from a trend.

Upon receipt of second quarter data for 2016, more accurate long term projections can be made due to the addition and availability of more monthly data and the potential for half year projections across quarters. It is also recommended that monthly sales data continue to be collected and aggregated throughout 2016 and 2017 in order to have three years of full data to analyze and test against this model.
